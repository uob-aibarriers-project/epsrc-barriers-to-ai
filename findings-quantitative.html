Overview

Quantitative Findings

Before conducting paired analyses, the initial dataset of 14 matched pre- and post-training surveys was cleaned to ensure data integrity. One participant was excluded for completing both surveys consecutively (two minutes apart). For a participant with multiple post-survey submissions, only the first complete submission was retained. This resulted in a final matched sample of n=13.

Two complementary scoring methods were applied to the 10-item AI-LITS test: (1) Raw Percentage — total correct answers out of 10; (2) IRT Ability Score (θ) — a 3-Parameter Logistic model using validated fixed item parameters from Hornberger et al. (2025), weighting questions by difficulty, discrimination, and adjusting for guessing probability.

H1

AI Training & Interest in AI Study

Figure 7: Pre- and post-training shifts in AI attitudes (n=13). Diverging stacked bar chart.

Shifting Attitudes: PhD Confidence

When asked to rate agreement with "I think I would be offered a place to study a PhD in AI if I applied" (1–5 scale), the median score remained static at 2.0 (Disagree) before and after training. The mean shifted from 2.15 to 2.54 — a weak trend approaching but not reaching statistical significance (W = 18.0, p = 0.094).

For direct willingness to apply for an AI PhD (0=No, 1=Maybe, 2=Yes), the median remained at 1.0 (Maybe). Mean rose slightly from 0.77 to 0.92, not statistically significant (W = 10.0, p = 0.375).

Key Interpretation

The diverging stacked bar chart reveals a marked increase in confidence to discuss AI, alongside positive directional trends in perceived PhD eligibility. However, a short technical intervention alone is insufficient to significantly alter deeply held career intentions or overcome confidence barriers regarding PhD admissions.

Key finding (H1)

While training successfully increased objective AI literacy (H2), the short technical intervention alone is insufficient to significantly alter deeply held career intentions or confidence barriers regarding PhD admissions.

H3

Socio-economic Status & AI Study Interest

Severe Demographic Skew

The self-selecting nature of training attendees revealed a severe demographic imbalance: 12 of the 13 matched participants fell into the Higher SES category, leaving only 1 participant in the Lower SES category. Due to this extreme imbalance, formal comparative hypothesis testing is impossible.

Whilst statistical conclusions cannot be drawn from a sample size of one, the fact that only a single first-generation student completed the AI training pipeline highlights a critical systemic issue regarding accessibility and early-stage engagement in AI education.

Lesson Learned

Future research should employ a purposive sampling strategy to actively recruit students from diverse socio-economic backgrounds. Engagement with AI training opportunities may already be unequal at the point of access.

H4

STEM vs. Non-STEM AI Literacy

Figure 10: Individual and group shifts in AI literacy by subject area. Slope chart comparing pre- and post-training scores for Non-STEM (n=7, left) and STEM (n=6, right) students.

The Diverging Trajectories

Prior to training, STEM students exhibited a higher baseline: mean raw score of 56.7% (θ = 0.251) vs. Non-STEM's 48.6% (θ = -0.041). Whilst both groups benefitted from the intervention, STEM students experienced substantially higher growth.

The training appears to act as a stronger catalyst for students already embedded in STEM disciplines, effectively widening the literacy gap rather than closing it — suggesting AI training may inadvertently reinforce existing inequalities.
