Research Methodology

We employed a pragmatic, sequential mixed-methods design — combining quantitative pre/post surveys with qualitative structured interviews — to explore socioeconomic and individual barriers to AI postgraduate study.

02

Sample & Recruitment

Data was collected from self-selecting participants drawn from students attending the "Getting Started in AI" training programme run by the Jean Golding Institute at the University of Bristol.

Eligibility Criteria

  • Aged 18 or over
  • Undergraduate students at the University of Bristol
  • Confirmed attendees of the "Getting Started in AI" training

To maximise participation, all confirmed attendees were contacted by email about the optional surveys, and time was allocated within sessions to complete them. The programme ran across two sessions, each delivered to two cohorts (A and B) due to room capacity constraints of 30 attendees per session. The research team supervised both cohorts and confirmed no significant differences in delivery between them.

04

Quantitative Surveys

A longitudinal pre- and post-test design tracked quantitative shifts in student attitudes and AI literacy before and after training. The pre-training survey was administered in the days before the programme; the post-training survey was offered after Session 1 with a two-week completion window.

Hypotheses Tested

  • H1 — Does the AI training programme increase attendees' interest in AI study?
  • H2 — Does the AI training programme increase attendees' AI literacy?
  • H3 — Is there any difference in interest in AI study based on SES?
  • H4 — Do STEM students show higher AI literacy than non-STEM students before and after training?

Instruments Used

  • AI-LITS (Short Version): The AI Literacy Scale (Hornberger et al., 2025) measured AI literacy pre- and post-training, with question order altered in the post survey to reduce recognition bias.
  • DAISY SES Indicators: Ethnic background, gender, caregivers' education, and current occupation — based on DAISY (2022) guidance and Census 2021 categories.
  • Likert Scales: Five-point agreement scales measured student attitudes towards using and studying AI, and motivation for attending the training.

Descriptive statistics were used to evaluate the intervention's impact: mean and range were compared for AI literacy scores; mode and range for agreement scale items. Demographic questions appeared only in the pre-training survey as these factors were not expected to change between surveys.

05

Structured Interviews

Survey participants were invited to attend structured online interviews (up to 60 mins) via Microsoft Teams following Session 2, guided by three qualitative research questions:

  • RQ1 — What do participants perceive as barriers and enablers for studying AI following the training?
  • RQ2 — How do career expectations influence their choices around postgraduate study after training?
  • RQ3 — How does institutional policy impact interest in AI studies after attending the training?

Structuring the interviews improved consistency across sessions conducted by different research team members. Each interviewer was accompanied by an observer who took field notes — allowing the interviewer to focus on inquiry — which were later used to compare similarity across interviewers. Interviews were automatically transcribed in Microsoft Teams and then reviewed and corrected by hand before analysis.

06

Data Analysis Strategy

The final phase synthesised both data streams to triangulate findings across quantitative and qualitative sources.

  • Thematic Analysis (Interviews): A deductive approach (Clarke & Braun, 2017) was followed due to time constraints. Codes were defined according to prior literature (Fife & Gossner, 2024) on AI policies, admission policies, AI literacy, and socioeconomic barriers, with additional codes derived from pilot interviews. Researchers worked in a shared codebook, reviewing codes asynchronously and in dedicated meetings.
  • Content Analysis (Survey Free-text): An abductive approach (Erlingsson & Brysiewicz, 2017) was applied to free-text survey responses. Predefined codes from the thematic analysis served as the deductive starting point; additional codes were derived from the data to enable frequency analysis across the dataset.
  • Quantitative: Descriptive statistics (mean, mode, range) compared pre- and post-training survey scores across AI literacy items and agreement scales.

Note: the JGI training focused on AI literacy rather than other barriers to doctoral study identified in the literature. This partial misalignment meant some codes (e.g., gender inequality, confidentiality concerns) were not addressed in post-training interviews, and admission policy questions could not be explored with an undergraduate sample.

Ethical Compliance & Incentives

Approved by the University of Bristol Faculty of Science and Engineering's Ethics Committee (Ref: 29619). We prioritised fair compensation for student time. The average pre-training survey took approximately 25 minutes; interviews lasted up to one hour.
