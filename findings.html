1. Market & Structural Findings

Institutional analysis of 2,382 active PhD listings in the UK.

The Funding Mirage

PhD funding is unevenly distributed and often opaque. Our analysis shows that 23.4% of listings do not provide explicit information on funding, creating a "transparency barrier" for students without independent financial support.

2. Qualitative Results

Technical Gatekeeping

"I never got any chance to study Python... it seems so complicated to a person from a social science major."

Interviews revealed that educational pathways in secondary school act as long-term filters. Non-STEM students perceive a "mathematical wall" that training interventions alone struggle to dismantle.

Figure 12: Tunnel Vision distribution—Most ads don't restrict degrees, but project scope filters backgrounds.

Career & Market Fears

"I chose my degree because it's broad... you always need a mechanical engineer. For AI, job opportunities seem limited."

International students expressed specific anxiety regarding visa sponsorship and market competitiveness. AI is seen as a tool for employability in current fields, rather than a new academic path.

Policy Perception

"Sometimes I feel a bit limited... just reading this policy [...] I'll always be thinking of, oh, what if there's a policy against what I'm doing?"

Institutional AI policies inadvertently create a "Constraint Effect" that stifles creative exploration outside of university contexts.

4. Conclusion

Research Questions Recapped

"H2: Does training increase literacy? YES (+16.2%)"

"H4: STEM vs Non-STEM difference? YES (STEM catalyst effect)"

"RQ2: Career expectations impact? YES ( employability focus > academic focus)"

The Summary

while training is effective in increasing AI literacy, it is not sufficient, in isolation, to transform longer-term educational pathways, particularly regarding postgraduate study.

Key Contributions

The Aspiration Gap

Enhanced literacy does not necessarily translate into PhD interest. Participants frame AI as a tool for employability within existing disciplines rather than a new academic field.

Reinforcing Inequalities

STEM students begin with higher baseline literacy and benefit more from training, suggesting technical interventions may inadvertently widen existing literacy divides.

Skewed Engagement

Minimal representation from lower socioeconomic backgrounds highlights that engagement inequality begins at the point of access, not just outcomes.

Future Directions & Recommendations

  • 1

    Contextualized Training: Technical labs alone are insufficient without explicit links to "real-world" disciplinary and career relevance.

  • 2

    Inclusive Recruitment: Future research must target underrepresented groups who aspired to PhDs but were unable to pursue them.

  • 3

    Longitudinal Design: Adopt long-term tracking to understand how AI perceptions evolve beyond the immediate post-intervention window.

"Addressing structural barriers requires a holistic approach that balances literacy development with systemic inclusivity."
