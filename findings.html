Chapter 1

Financial Accessibility

Analysis of recruitment data shows that PhD funding is uneven and uncertain. While almost half of the PhD listings include a funding opportunity, many require self-funding or competitive applications — introducing additional barriers for prospective candidates.

"The financial burden of completing a PhD is a substantial barrier to entry into postgraduate research programmes, especially in a resource-intensive field like AI."

Figure 1: Funding status among PhD listings.

1.1 The Funding Split

Only 48.6% of listings provide direct project funding. A significant 21.5% require students to enter university competitions, where funding is allocated at the faculty level rather than tied to a specific project. Although such competitions often provide full fee coverage and stipends, they introduce an additional layer of uncertainty for prospective candidates.

Furthermore, 23.4% of listings do not provide explicit information on funding arrangements, leaving applicants unable to assess the financial feasibility of pursuing the position. In a highly competitive field such as AI, this lack of transparency may deter candidates without independent financial support.

Figure 2: Advertised annual PhD stipends.

1.2 Stipend Benchmarks

Adverts cluster heavily around the UKRI minimum of £20,780, confirming that when funding is openly advertised, it aligns with standard UKRI guidelines alongside expected adjustments for London-based institutions. UKRI announced a ~5% increase for students starting 2026/27, just after data collection.

Figure 3: Presence of explicit financial top-ups.

1.3 The Top-Up Gap

Only 30.3% of listings mention additional funding for training, conferences, and research dissemination. Industrial partner projects may offer up to £10,000 top-ups, while others provide only £1,000 per annum — inadequate given international conference fees exceeding £300.

Figure 4: Top-up availability by institution.

1.4 Institutional Disparities

Top-up availability varies widely across institutions. The University of Bristol sits near the middle at approximately 22%, highlighting significant variation in additional research support across the doctoral landscape.

Figure 5: Clarity of funding within funded PhD listings.

1.5 Ambiguity as a Filter

Among funded listings, 84.2% include explicit statements of secured funding. However, 172 listings remain vague, and 9 are conditional — deterring applicants without institutional insider knowledge of university funding mechanisms.

Who Pays for AI PhDs?

The most common contributors are host Universities (630 listings) and UKRI Research Councils (604 listings). The EPSRC accounts for approximately 9.6% of analysed listings. Direct industry and charity contributions remain comparatively low.

Chapter 2

Global Ambition, National Restrictions

Despite the advertised openness of UK PhD listings, a systemic financial trap emerges for international applicants. Institutional ambition collides with national funding policy, leaving overseas talent systematically priced out.

706 projects invite international applicants but cap funding at Home-only rates — forcing students to self-fund a shortfall of roughly £15,000 per year.

Figure 7: Eligibility breakdown by student fee status.

2.1 The Advertised Openness

Fee coverage for funded roles is split almost evenly between Home-only (829) and International (821) students. A high degree of advertised openness exists: 2,262 listings do not explicitly restrict applications to UK nationals. However, this surface openness conceals a deep structural barrier.

UKRI rules permit up to 30% of a cohort to be international students, but cap financial awards at the domestic fee rate. Unless the host university absorbs the difference, international applicants are legally permitted to apply but systematically priced out — forced to self-fund a tuition shortfall of roughly £15,000 per year.

Chapter 3

Lesser Grade Pressure

It is often presumed that pursuing postgraduate studies requires a First-Class degree. However, most universities now accept candidates with a 2:1, suggesting a broad institutional shift away from rigid academic exceptionalism at the point of entry.

Formal degree requirements may not reflect informal shortlisting practices or competitive dynamics among applicants.

Figure 9: Academic grade requirements across AI PhD listings — a comparison of formal degree expectations across universities.

3.1 Softening Entry Standards

The overwhelming dominance of the unspecified degree requirement (grey bar) and broad acceptance of 2:1 degrees illustrates a broad institutional shift. Only a small minority of listings explicitly demand First-Class honours, suggesting that formal grade-based gatekeeping is declining.

We must be cautious however: the absence of strict formal requirements does not necessarily reflect informal shortlisting practices at the institutional level, including supervisory preferences or competitive dynamics among applicants.

Figure 10: Acceptance of equivalent professional experience — showing that nearly two-thirds of listings strictly require formal academic degrees.

3.2 Limited Alternative Pathways

Beyond degree classifications, alternative routes into doctoral study remain limited. Only around 34.2% of listings explicitly recognise equivalent practical or professional experience as a valid basis for assessing research suitability.

Despite some movement towards flexibility in formal requirements, academic credentials continue to dominate as the primary mechanism for evaluating candidates. The majority of listings — 65.8% — require a degree only, with no recognition of professional equivalence.

Chapter 4

Application Processes and Diversity Initiatives

Figure 11: Prevalence of elitist language by institution — average buzzwords per listing.

4.1 Prestige Signalling

Elitist keywords such as references to "world-class" institutions or "leading" research environments are relatively uncommon overall. This suggests that symbolic signalling of prestige is not a dominant feature of AI PhD listings, though a small subset of universities — notably Sheffield and Durham — stand out with notably higher average buzzwords per listing.

Figure 12: Pre-application networking requirements — listings requiring supervisor contact before applying vs open applications.

4.2 The Networking Hurdle

A subset of universities requires students to contact supervisors before applying. While often framed as early engagement, such requirements can create a barrier for candidates with limited prior academic networks or confidence in approaching senior academics — functioning as an additional gatekeeping mechanism for those without social capital.

Figure 13: Presence and framing of diversity initiatives — None, Tier B (Passive), and Tier A (Active).

4.3 Passive Inclusion Dominates

We examined the presence and framing of diversity initiatives within PhD listings, distinguishing between active diversity language (concrete measures such as guaranteed interviews or targeted bursaries) and passive diversity language (generic encouragements with no actionable support).

The vast majority of listings — over 1,300 — contain no diversity statement. Of those that do, passive Tier B language (~800 listings) far outweighs active Tier A provisions (~200 listings). This absence is striking given the growing institutional emphasis on equality, diversity and inclusion in UK higher education.

Notable gap: Across the entire dataset, we found no explicit references to neurodiversity — suggesting that neurodivergent applicants remain largely invisible within the recruitment discourse of AI PhD programmes.

Chapter 5

Gender Representation

The Visible "Gender Void"

The data reveals a stark gender void in AI supervision. The market is dominated by presumed all-male supervisory teams, accounting for the largest single category at ~38%. Research confirms that the lack of visible female role models significantly lowers AI self-efficacy of female applicants, potentially causing them to self-select out of the field before they even apply.

0% explicit mention of neurodiversity support across the entire dataset.

Chapter 6

Discipline Requirement

Figure 15: Disciplinary framing across institutions — Tunnel Vision vs Broad eligibility.

6.1 Disciplinary Openness

Contrary to common assumptions, AI PhD programmes are relatively open in disciplinary framing. Only 96 openings were classified as exhibiting explicit "disciplinary tunnel vision," where eligibility was restricted to a narrow set of technical degrees. Most projects appear open on paper, yet they filter candidates through implied technical competencies embedded within project descriptions.

6.2 The Python Barrier

Python is the undisputed gatekeeper language with over 400 listings. 22.4% of listings explicitly mandate coding proficiency, signalling a shift from formal degrees to "assumed technical fluency." This suggests a transition from overt disciplinary exclusion to a system structured around assumed computational capital, which may differentially disadvantage applicants from non-technical backgrounds.

Chapter 6 Summary

Taken together, AI PhD listings in the UK are relatively open in formal disciplinary framing. Explicit restrictions to computer science or mathematics degrees are rare, and most projects do not impose hard technical requirements. However, this formal inclusivity coexists with implicit skill-based filtering — programming competence and familiarity with computational frameworks are frequently embedded within project descriptions. Access is shaped less by degree classification and more by prior exposure to technical practices, reinforcing the core rationale behind AI literacy interventions like the JGI training programme.
