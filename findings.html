Comprehensive Research Findings

Bridging structural analysis of 2,643 PhD listings with the impact of literacy interventions and qualitative student experiences.

1. Structural Barriers & Market Analysis

Analysis of 2,382 unique active PhD listings in the UK.

1.1 Financial Accessibility

PhD funding is unevenly distributed. While roughly half of listings offer direct funding, a significant portion (23.1%) remains self-funded or dependent on competitive institutional rounds, creating high uncertainty for applicants.

Key Data Point

76.9% of analyzed roles are funded studentships, while 23.1% are unfunded.

Figure 1: Funding status distribution across UK AI PhD listings.

1.2 Stipend Alignment

Adverts largely align with UKRI minimum guidelines, with adjustments for London-based institutions.

1.3 The "Top-Up" Gap

Only 30.3% of adverts mention additional funding for professional development, training, or conferences.

1.4 Institutional Disparities

Top-up availability varies significantly across the UK. Bristol sits near the middle at approximately 22%.

1.5 Ambiguity as a Barrier

84.2% of positions offer secured support, but 15% remain vaguely defined, deterring applicants without insider knowledge.

Figure 6: Shift towards accepting 2:1 degrees over first-class requirements.

1.6 Evolving Academic Exceptionalism

There is a visible shift away from requiring a First-Class degree. However, formal flexibility is countered by limited recognition of professional experience (Figure 7).

Figure 7: Only 35% of roles explicitly recognize practical experience.

Prestige Signaling

Elite keywords like "world-class" are relatively uncommon, suggesting symbolic prestige is not the primary gatekeeper.

Social Capital Barrier

Requirements to contact supervisors before applying create a hurdle for those without established academic networks.

Passive Inclusion

Diversity statements are often passive encouragements rather than active support frameworks.

1.7 The Gender and Skill Divide

Our analysis reveals a stark "Gender Void" in AI supervision, dominated by male-only teams. Furthermore, there is a clear transition from disciplinary exclusion to implicit technical filtering based on programming fluency.

2. Impact of AI Literacy Training

Quantitative shifts in ability and attitudes following intervention.

2.1 Shifting Attitudes

The intervention led to a marked increase in students' confidence to hold informed conversations about AI, though career intentions remained more static.

2.2 Measurable Knowledge Gains

Mean raw literacy scores surged by 16.2%, shifting from 52.3% to 68.5%. The IRT model confirmed a 0.5 SD shift in true ability.

2.3 The Subject Area Catalyst

While both groups benefited, the training acted as a stronger catalyst for STEM students (+21.7pp) compared to Non-STEM students (+11.4pp), highlighting the need for tailored support to close the literacy gap.

3. Qualitative Dimensions

Themes extracted from participant interviews and feedback.

Technical Gatekeeping

"I never got any chance to study Python... it seems so complicated to a person from a social science major."

Educational pathways in high school act as long-term filters, creating a perceived mathematical barrier that discourages entry into AI PhD programs.

Career Expectations

"I chose my degree because it's broad... you always need a mechanical engineer. For AI, job opportunities seem limited."

Ambiguity about non-technical career paths and fear of market competitiveness for migrants remain major psychological barriers.
