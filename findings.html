Mixed-Methods Research

Mapping the Evidence

From institutional market analysis of 2,382 listings to quantifying the impact of AI literacy training. Our data reveals a landscape of structural ambiguity and significant potential for growth.

Chapter 1

Financial Accessibility

Analysis of recruitment data shows that PhD funding is uneven and uncertain. While almost half of the PhD listings include a funding opportunity, many require self-funding or competitive applications — introducing additional barriers for prospective candidates.

"The financial burden of completing a PhD is a substantial barrier to entry into postgraduate research programs, especially in a resource-intensive field like AI."

Figure 1: Funding status among PhD listings.

1.1 The Funding Split

Only 48.6% of listings provide direct project funding. A significant 21.5% require students to enter university competitions, where funding is allocated at the faculty level rather than tied to a specific project. Although such competitions often provide full fee coverage and stipends, they introduce an additional layer of uncertainty for prospective candidates.

Furthermore, 23.4% of listings do not provide explicit information on funding arrangements, leaving applicants unable to assess the financial feasibility of pursuing the position. In a highly competitive field such as AI, this lack of transparency may deter candidates without independent financial support.

Figure 2: Advertised annual PhD stipends.

1.2 Stipend Benchmarks

Adverts cluster heavily around the UKRI minimum of £20,780, confirming that when funding is openly advertised, it aligns with standard UKRI guidelines alongside expected adjustments for London-based institutions. UKRI announced a ~5% increase for students starting 2026/27, just after data collection.

Figure 3: Presence of explicit financial top-ups.

1.3 The Top-Up Gap

Only 30.3% of listings mention additional funding for training, conferences, and research dissemination. Industrial partner projects may offer up to £10,000 top-ups, while others provide only £1,000 per annum — inadequate given international conference fees exceeding £300.

Figure 4: Top-up availability by institution.

1.4 Institutional Disparities

The availability of research support varies widely across institutions. The University of Bristol sits near the middle (~22%), highlighting significant variation in the support landscape for doctoral researchers.

Figure 5: Clarity of funding within funded PhD listings.

1.5 Ambiguity as a Filter

Among funded listings, 84.2% include explicit statements of secured funding. However, 172 listings remain vague, and 9 are conditional. This ambiguity deters applicants without institutional insider knowledge of university funding mechanisms.

Primary Funding Sources

Who Pays for AI PhDs?

The most common contributors are host Universities (630 listings) and UKRI Research Councils (604 listings). The EPSRC accounts for approximately 9.6% of analysed listings. Direct industry and charity contributions remain comparatively low.

The International Paywall

While 2,262 listings do not restrict applications to UK nationals, 706 projects invite international students but strictly cap funding at the "Home Only" fee level — forcing applicants to self-fund a shortfall of roughly £15,000 per year. This is largely driven by UKRI policies permitting up to 30% international cohorts but capping financial awards at domestic rates.

Chapter 2

Academic Gatekeeping & Symbols

Is AI an institutionally closed domain? Our analysis shows a shift from rigid grade requirements to "implicit" technical filters and a visible gender void in leadership. Despite some softening of formal barriers, informal mechanisms persist.

Figure 6: Academic grade requirements across AI PhD listings.

2.1 Softening Grade Pressure

Increasing institutional acceptance of 2:1 degrees suggests a broad move away from extreme academic exceptionalism at the entry level, though informal selection mechanisms and disciplinary hierarchies persist.

Figure 7: Acceptance of equivalent professional experience.

2.2 The Professional Gap

Only ~35% of listings explicitly value prior professional experience, indicating that alternative pathways into doctoral study remain limited and academic credentials continue to dominate as the primary evaluation mechanism.

2.3 The Paywall Trap

Elitist Language

References to "world-class" institutions are relatively uncommon overall, suggesting that symbolic signalling of prestige is not a dominant feature of AI PhD listings.

Figure 8: Prevalence of elitist language by institution.

2.4 Networking Hurdle

Social Capital

A subset of universities requires students to contact supervisors before applying, which can act as a gatekeeping mechanism for applicants without established academic networks.

EDI Framing

Diversity statements are overwhelmingly passive "encouragements" rather than actionable support frameworks. No listings contain explicit references to neurodiversity support.

Our analysis of 1,065 UK AI PhD ads confirms that supervisory leadership remains overwhelmingly male-dominated. The market is dominated by presumed male-only supervisory teams. Research confirms that the lack of visible female role models significantly lowers AI self-efficacy of female applicants, potentially causing them to self-select out of the field before they even apply.

Chapter 3 Summary

Taken together, AI PhD listings in the UK are relatively open in formal disciplinary framing, but this formal inclusivity coexists with implicit skill-based filtering. Programming competence and familiarity with computational frameworks are frequently embedded within project descriptions. Access is shaped less by degree classification and more by prior exposure to technical practices — reinforcing the core rationale behind AI literacy interventions like the JGI training programme.

Qualitative Results

Methods

Qualitative Analysis

A deductive thematic analysis approach was applied to interview transcripts (Clarke and Braun, 2017). Codes were defined according to prior literature on AI policies, admission policies, AI literacy, and socioeconomic barriers. Abductive content analysis was used for free-text survey responses.

Thematic Analysis

Applied to interview transcripts. Codes derived from prior literature and pilot interviews, reviewed asynchronously and in dedicated team meetings.

Content Analysis

Abductive content analysis of free-text survey responses (Humble and Mozelius, 2022). Frequency analysis enabled efficient categorisation across the dataset.

⚠ Important Caveat

The JGI training focused on AI literacy rather than all barriers identified in the literature. Some codes (e.g., gender inequality, confidentiality) were not addressed in post-training interviews. Additionally, the undergraduate sample constrained analysis of doctoral admission policies.

RQ1

AI Literacy: Barriers & Enablers

1.1 Technical Skills Acquisition

Technical skills were the most frequently mentioned factor influencing participants' engagement with AI studies. Knowledge of concepts such as LLMs and neural networks had a positive influence on attitudes toward engaging with AI as users within related disciplines.

Having prior coding skills and experience helped participants feel more confident in expanding their AI knowledge and considering further studies. Participants particularly appreciated having AI substreams broken down for an easier learning experience.

Conversely, having little or no coding background and limited AI knowledge was a factor that made participants less willing to further explore the training, let alone develop interest in AI postgraduate studies.

"So the way I use LLMs was also affected in terms of realising and understanding how something works. You know how to better use it based on its underlying mechanism."

Theme: Technical Understanding

"Maybe it's because of like I don't have that technical background of it. So like I got the base of it, but still like I'm not — probably I won't go for an AI postgrad."

Theme: Technical Barrier

"Back in my high school... I took like Politics, Geography, History and Economics. I never got any chance to like study Python... because it seems so complicated to a person from a social science major."

Theme: Educational Pathway

1.2 Capacity Building & Training Accessibility

Participants highlighted that being part of the university allowed them to access training programmes that better introduced them to AI. They appreciated both the technical content of the "Getting Started in AI" training and its accessibility — being free, friendly, and easy to attend.

Even though training was not a decisive factor in pursuing AI studies for most, it offered awareness of the field. Self-efficacy improved from 36% (pre-training) to 63% (post-training) in free-text responses, consistent with prior literature on confidence and AI engagement (Cowit and Fiesler, 2024).

Notable gap: Reflections on accessibility did not extend to minorities or disadvantaged groups, contextualised within the demographic biases of the sample studied.

"I also took the course to kind of help me decide whether I'd like to pursue a Masters of Science in AI rather than Masters of Science and Engineering, which was my original path."

Theme: Decision-Making Support

"Being invited through email and posters, promotion of a free training, and being in a friendly environment encourages people to engage with AI more."

Theme: Accessibility
RQ2

Career Expectations & Postgraduate Choices

2.1 Relevance to Current Pathway

For some participants, engaging with AI as a user of systems was more important than studying AI itself. The application of AI was not clear to them in fields like social sciences and marketing. This aligns with Yin and Goh's (2024) finding that a key driver for learning AI is utility rather than pure technical interest.

"I don't know if taking a career, but learning more about it, getting more into more courses like this one."

2.2 Future Career Ambiguity

All participants discussed career expectations as central to their decisions. Ambiguity about future careers was one of the main factors influencing decisions about AI postgraduate study. Concerns varied between field competitiveness, uncertainty about required expertise, and how AI connects to non-STEM disciplines.

"I chose [this degree] because... I thought it's very broad here in England and I can go into any industry that I want. They will always need a mechanical engineer for something."

Theme: Career Security

"There's always the risk of something being true right now, but then completely changing in a year's time because of the nature of the field where everything is changing rapidly."

Theme: Field Volatility

Rapid AI change creates uncertainty about whether skills remain relevant — a key psychological barrier to long-term commitment.

"And actually, one of the guys [delivering the training] was [from abroad], so we had a chat in [their home language]... I'm not from England, so that's really good to know that anyone can get into it."

Theme: Intersectionality

Implies perception that AI careers in the UK may be primarily open to UK nationals — pointing to intersectionality of race/ethnicity/migration and career pathway decisions.

RQ3

Institutional Policy Impact

3.1 Perceptions of UoB AI Policy

Most interviewed participants found the UoB AI policy to be clear. The examples provided in the policy for the limits of AI use offered practical guidance. However, familiarity with the policy varied significantly — those who had engaged with the guidebook were more positive about its clarity.

The exposure to the policy through lecturers, emails, and personal curiosity influenced understanding. Responses fell on a spectrum from "too strict" to "a fair policy that respects academic integrity without inhibiting AI use."

Key finding: There were no indications that the UoB AI policy discourages students from pursuing a postgraduate degree in AI.

"Seeing that it had been considered by the university as well and it wasn't that black or white, yes or no type of situation, encouraged me. I was happy to see that."

Theme: Policy Clarity

"Sometimes I feel a bit limited because, for coursework obviously I'm not going to use it like to copy and paste... So even if I want to use it for personal use... I'll always be thinking of, oh, what if there's a policy against what I'm doing?"

Theme: Over-generalised Restriction

Limitation: This study cannot claim a thorough and structured policy evaluation. Admission policies related to doctoral studies in AI could not be investigated due to sampling constraints (undergraduate sample). The literature on admission policies addresses these topics more thoroughly.

Free-Text Survey: Content Analysis

Content analysis of free-text survey responses revealed the frequency of topics mentioned pre- and post-training across three prompts: (A) Interest in studying AI, (B) Interest in undertaking a PhD, (C) Motivations for / experiences of the JGI training.

Figure 11: Bar chart displaying the percentage of responses featuring various factors that interest participants in studying AI (pre- vs. post-training).

Interest in Studying AI

The most frequently stated motivator before training was the "Importance or Risks of AI" (64% pre-training, dropping to 40% post-training) — reflecting concern that AI is necessary for future-proofing careers. After training, "Specific Applications of AI" rose from 35% to 60%, and "Career Prospects" rose from 11% to 50%, suggesting engagement became more orientated toward skills acquisition and specific career applications.

"Programming" was mentioned by 29% pre-training and 30% post-training. "Capacity Building" rose significantly from 17% to 30%. The shift indicates that as a result of the training, participants' interest in AI became more concrete and career-focused rather than driven by general anxiety about AI's importance.

Figure 12: Bar chart displaying topics related to participants' interest in undertaking a PhD by percentage of responses (pre- vs. post-training).

Interest in Undertaking a PhD

PhD motivations were more closely aligned with knowledge acquisition and personal interests than AI interest per se. The "Pursuit of Knowledge" was mentioned by 66% pre-training and 71% post-training. "Work or Learning Preferences" (preference for long-term, high-level projects) featured in 33% pre- and 28% post-training responses.

Notably, "Career Prospects" as a PhD motivation rose from 11% to 42% post-training — distinct from career anxiety, reflecting a recognition of a PhD as a deliberate career pathway choice rather than a necessity.

Figure 13: Bar chart displaying the frequency of topics in responses about motivations for (pre-) and experiences of (post-) taking part in the Getting Started in AI training.

Training Session Motivations & Reflections

Initial motivations for attending were driven by desires to develop technical understanding ("Understanding of AI Concepts", 68%) and "future-proofing." Self-Efficacy increased from 36% to 63% between pre- and post-training surveys — students reflected on the training as a starting point for increasing confidence rather than a final destination.

"Access to AI Training Opportunities" rose from 21% to 45%. "Work or Learning Preferences" (appreciation for practical learning) rose from 21% to 54%. "Programming Skills" jumped from 10% to 36%, consistent with the technical focus of the training syllabus.

Quantitative Results

Overview

Quantitative Findings

Before conducting paired analyses, the initial dataset of 14 matched pre- and post-training surveys was cleaned to ensure data integrity. One participant was excluded for completing both surveys consecutively (two minutes apart). For a participant with multiple post-survey submissions, only the first complete submission was retained. This resulted in a final matched sample of n=13.

Two complementary scoring methods were applied to the 10-item AI-LITS test: (1) Raw Percentage — total correct answers out of 10; (2) IRT Ability Score (θ) — a 3-Parameter Logistic model using validated fixed item parameters from Hornberger et al. (2025), weighting questions by difficulty, discrimination, and adjusting for guessing probability.

H1

AI Training & Interest in AI Study

Figure 7: Pre- and post-training shifts in AI attitudes (n=13). Diverging stacked bar chart.

Shifting Attitudes: PhD Confidence

When asked to rate agreement with "I think I would be offered a place to study a PhD in AI if I applied" (1–5 scale), the median score remained static at 2.0 (Disagree) before and after training. The mean shifted from 2.15 to 2.54 — a weak trend approaching but not reaching statistical significance (W = 18.0, p = 0.094).

For direct willingness to apply for an AI PhD (0=No, 1=Maybe, 2=Yes), the median remained at 1.0 (Maybe). Mean rose slightly from 0.77 to 0.92, not statistically significant (W = 10.0, p = 0.375).

Key Interpretation

The diverging stacked bar chart reveals a marked increase in confidence to discuss AI, alongside positive directional trends in perceived PhD eligibility. However, a short technical intervention alone is insufficient to significantly alter deeply held career intentions or overcome confidence barriers regarding PhD admissions.

Key finding (H1)

While training successfully increased objective AI literacy (H2), the short technical intervention alone is insufficient to significantly alter deeply held career intentions or confidence barriers regarding PhD admissions.

H3

Socioeconomic Status & AI Study Interest

Severe Demographic Skew

The self-selecting nature of training attendees revealed a severe demographic imbalance: 12 of the 13 matched participants fell into the Higher SES category, leaving only 1 participant in the Lower SES category. Due to this extreme imbalance, formal comparative hypothesis testing is impossible.

While statistical conclusions cannot be drawn from a sample size of one, the fact that only a single first-generation student completed the AI training pipeline highlights a critical systemic issue regarding accessibility and early-stage engagement in AI education.

Lesson Learned

Future research should employ a purposive sampling strategy to actively recruit students from diverse socioeconomic backgrounds. Engagement with AI training opportunities may already be unequal at the point of access.

H4

STEM vs. Non-STEM AI Literacy

The Diverging Trajectories

Prior to training, STEM students exhibited a higher baseline: mean raw score of 56.7% (θ = 0.251) vs. Non-STEM's 48.6% (θ = -0.041). While both groups benefitted from the intervention, STEM students experienced substantially higher growth.

The training appears to act as a stronger catalyst for students already embedded in STEM disciplines, effectively widening the literacy gap rather than closing it — suggesting AI training may inadvertently reinforce existing inequalities.

Mixed-Methods Evaluation

Key Conclusions

This study provides a mixed-methods evaluation of a short AI training delivered by the Jean Golding Institute (University of Bristol, UK), offering insight into how AI training may shape students' AI literacy, perceptions, and, potentially, educational aspirations. The main aim is to understand whether AI training may impact real and/or perceived barriers to AI doctoral studies. By combining survey data, qualitative interviews, and content analysis, the findings highlight a consistent pattern: while training is effective in increasing AI knowledge/literacy, it is not sufficient, in isolation, to transform longer-term educational pathways, particularly regarding postgraduate study.

Literacy Gains

Quantitative findings demonstrate a clear improvement in AI literacy following training — supported by both raw scores (+16.2pp, p=0.024) and IRT-based measures (+0.5 SD).

Confidence as User

Qualitative insights show that increased familiarity with concepts enhances confidence in engaging with AI — primarily as users within existing disciplines, rather than toward a potential PhD.

Employability Frame

AI literacy enhancement does not necessarily translate into increased interest in AI postgraduate study. Participants frame AI engagement as a means of improving employability, not as an academic pathway.

A key contribution is identifying the gap between knowledge acquisition and postgraduate educational aspirations. Across qualitative data, three interrelated factors emerge: the perceived relevance of AI to one's current field, confidence in technical skills (particularly coding), and uncertainty about career pathways in AI. For non-STEM students, AI continues to be perceived as implying a shift in competencies, limiting willingness to pursue further AI study despite learning outcomes.

The Inequality Risk

In contrast, STEM students not only begin with higher baseline literacy but may also benefit more from the training, suggesting that AI training may inadvertently reinforce existing inequalities rather than reducing them. The highly skewed sample — with minimal representation from lower socioeconomic backgrounds — highlights that engagement with AI training opportunities may already be unequal at the point of access.

This study also shows the impact of contextualisation. Participants frequently expressed uncertainty about how AI is linked to their discipline and career aspirations, suggesting that technical training alone may not be sufficient without clear links to real-world career and disciplinary relevance.

Limitations

  • —Small sample (n=13) limits generalisability and constrains statistical analysis, particularly for socioeconomic differences.
  • —Short timeframe between pre- and post-training measures means only immediate effects could be measured; no long-term follow-up.
  • —Undergraduate sample limits the ability to explore questions related to AI doctoral study and admissions policies.
  • —SES skew: 12/13 participants from Higher SES — formal H3 hypothesis testing was not possible.
  • —Gender inference via LLM relies on probabilistic name associations, not self-identification, risking misclassification.

Future Directions

  • +Longitudinal design to better understand how AI engagement evolves over time and across diverse populations.
  • +Purposive sampling targeting underrepresented groups and current/prospective PhD candidates who aspired to AI studies but were unable to proceed.
  • +Training with contextualisation — explicitly highlighting career and disciplinary applications of AI to support sustained engagement.
  • +Intersectional analysis of race/ethnicity/migration and career pathway decisions, which emerged as a theme warranting further investigation.

Summary Statement

This study demonstrates that AI training may be an effective tool for AI literacy, but it does not, in isolation, address the broader barriers shaping students' engagement with AI as a field of study. Addressing structural and institutional barriers to AI doctoral studies requires a more holistic approach that takes into account literacy development and contextualisation, while also considering inclusivity and support for diverse learners.
